Diversity in India

India is a land of immense cultural, linguistic, and religious diversity. It is home to over 1,600 languages and multiple religions like Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, and Jainism. Each region of India has its own unique traditions, clothing, food, and festivals. Despite differences, Indians celebrate each other's festivals with unity and harmony. The diversity extends to geography too — from snow-covered mountains to deserts, beaches, and rainforests. Art, music, and dance vary widely across states, creating a rich and colorful cultural fabric. The Indian Constitution recognizes and protects this diversity, promoting unity in diversity. This multiculturalism makes India one of the most vibrant and pluralistic democracies in the world.

Capital Of India

The capital of India is New Delhi, officially declared as the capital in 1931 during British rule. It is part of the National Capital Territory (NCT) of Delhi, which includes Old Delhi and other surrounding areas. New Delhi serves as the political and administrative center of India. It houses important institutions like Rashtrapati Bhavan (President's House), Parliament House, and the Supreme Court. Designed by British architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker, the city is known for its planned layout and colonial architecture. It is a hub for government, international diplomacy, and national events. New Delhi also reflects India’s rich history, culture, and modern development. The city is well-connected with modern infrastructure, including metro rail, airports, and roadways.

Currency In India

The official currency of India is the Indian Rupee (₹), and its currency code is INR. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is responsible for issuing and regulating Indian currency. Indian Rupee notes are available in denominations of ₹10, ₹20, ₹50, ₹100, ₹200, ₹500, and ₹2000. Coins are available in denominations of ₹1, ₹2, ₹5, ₹10, and sometimes 50 paise. Each note features Mahatma Gandhi’s image on the front. Modern banknotes come with advanced security features like watermarks and color-shifting ink. The rupee is not freely convertible for capital account transactions but is widely used for trade and travel. The symbol ₹ was officially adopted in 2010, designed to reflect Indian and Latin script elements.
